02 — The three opportunities

What I'd build, and
exactly how

Each concept below is paired with a mid-fidelity wireframe sketch showing the proposed UI, and an intern-level execution note explaining how I'd actually approach it in the first 90 days.

01
Trust & Safety — Critical Priority
Biometric avatar lock: your body, only yours

The most pressing unresolved risk in the entire VTO category. Zara's December 2025 rollout immediately triggered privacy controversy — users worried about non-consensual image use. For Doji, this is existential: a social app where people share avatar looks is only safe if those avatars cannot be created using someone else's image.

The fix: liveness detection during avatar creation. A brief "blink once, turn slightly left" prompt that verifies the selfie is live and belongs to the person consenting. This already exists in banking and identity verification contexts — it's not new technology, it's a new application of existing tech in fashion.

As intern: Run a qualitative research study specifically with women users asking about their comfort level sharing avatars publicly. Bring findings to the team as a safety brief before any social sharing feature ships. The feature doesn't need to be built to make the case for building it.
Mid-Fidelity Wireframe · Concept 01
Avatar Creation Flow — Biometric Verification Step
A new step inserted between "take your selfie" and "generate avatar" — live face verification that is invisible when done right, critical when absent.
The liveness check adds ~15 seconds to onboarding. User research will tell us if that's acceptable friction — my hypothesis is yes, especially when framed as "protecting your avatar."
02
Market Expansion — Untapped $65B Opportunity
South Asian fashion — the biggest gap in VTO

I'm Pakistani. Every try-on tool I've used fails on South Asian garments. Dupatta draping logic, kurta length variations, lehenga volume — these require a completely different garment physics model than Western structured separates. The AI hasn't been trained on this.

The market: South Asian fashion is estimated at $65B+ annually, with designers like Sabyasachi, Raw Mango, and Faraz Manan building genuinely global audiences. India's VTO market alone is projected to grow at 30%+ CAGR to 2030 — the fastest of any geography. And no Western VTO platform has meaningfully entered this space. It is completely uncontested.

As intern: Document current avatar failure modes with South Asian garments — film it, show the team. Then identify 3 South Asian designer brands as potential pilot partners and write a one-page brief on why a Sabyasachi-style partnership would be as strategically significant for Doji as Peter Do was.

03
Product Depth — Build on What's Already There
Look Studio exists. Now make it work for everyone.

Doji already launched Look Studio — shoes, bags, accessories, 1M+ products from designer brands. That's the right call. Styling a full look, not just a top, is exactly how people actually shop. The instinct is correct. What Look Studio needs now isn't more products — it's a foundation strong enough to make the output trustworthy.

Right now, users can style anything on an avatar that doesn't look like them. The person who came to Doji to try on a Bottega bag alongside her coat can do that — but if her avatar makes her look two sizes smaller, she still can't trust the result. Look Studio built the surface. Concept 04 (avatar body fidelity) builds the foundation underneath it. Both need to exist for either to deliver on their promise.

There's also a discovery gap. One reviewer put it directly: a "huge list of brands" with no guidance on which to try. The most valuable upgrade to Look Studio isn't another category — it's a smarter recommendation layer. An AI-powered "Complete the Look" panel that suggests specific accessory pairings based on what the user already has on the avatar. That moves Look Studio from a catalogue you browse to a styling tool that thinks with you.

As intern: Start with a Look Studio QA audit — try on as many combinations as possible, document every rendering failure, categorise by garment type. Build the evidence base for prioritisation. Then propose a focused pilot: one accessory category (sunglasses — Doji's face geometry is already captured), one brand partner, one user cohort. Ship, measure, bring the conversion data back to the roadmap discussion.
Mid-Fidelity Wireframe · Concept 03
Look Studio — AI Complete the Look Panel
The existing Look Studio canvas extended with an AI suggestion panel that drives cross-category discovery. The key upgrade isn't more products — it's smarter pairing logic.
The "AI Complete the Look" panel transforms Look Studio from a catalogue you scroll into a stylist that makes suggestions. That's what turns browsing into buying — and it's the upgrade that makes 1M+ products feel curated rather than overwhelming.
04
Core Product Quality — The Foundation Under Look Studio
The avatar should look like you, not a slimmer version of you

This one came directly from real users. App Store reviews say it plainly: the avatar makes everyone look skinnier than they are, which undermines the entire premise — including Look Studio. If I can't trust that the avatar is actually me, I can't trust how the clothes look on it. Look Studio built a beautiful surface. This is the foundation underneath it.

The fix has two layers. First: body sculpting tools at avatar creation — let users pinch and pull to match their actual proportions. Not just height and weight sliders, but real body geometry: shoulder width, belly curve, hip-to-waist ratio. Women with PCOS carry weight differently than someone with the same BMI. Someone petite with a fuller chest doesn't fit the same top as someone taller. The avatar needs to hold that nuance.

Second: garment adjustment controls. Clothes don't sit the same way on every body — and users know this. A miniskirt turning into a maxi on the avatar (another review complaint) is a model failure. Beyond fixing that bug, there's a real opportunity to let users actively style: shorten a hemline, adjust how a blouse tucks, change the drape of a scarf. The same top worn open vs. buttoned is a completely different look. Doji could be the first platform to let you experiment with that before you buy.

As intern: Structured audit of App Store and Play Store reviews, categorising every mention of avatar accuracy into body representation issues vs. garment rendering issues. That audit becomes a brief. The brief becomes a backlog. I'd push for at least one body geometry improvement to ship within the internship window — even just adding belly/hip curve controls would meaningfully expand the user base who feel seen by the avatar. And it would make Look Studio's 1M+ products actually worth trusting.

03 — Business case

How Doji becomes
a revenue engine

Most consumer apps pick one monetization model. Doji is in a position to build multiple revenue streams that are structurally complementary — each one strengthening the others. This is the McKinsey framing: understand the value creation flywheel before choosing where to extract from it.

The Doji Value Flywheel
How each element reinforces the next
◈
Affiliate Commission
8–12%
On every purchase made through Doji. Fashion affiliate programs average 8–15% for luxury brands (Nordstrom, Khaite, etc.). With 10,000 MAU making a single $400 purchase/month = $3.2M–4.8M ARR from commissions alone. This scales linearly with GMV.
◇
Brand Universe Fees
$25k–$200k
Exclusive drops like Peter Do's PD-168. Brands pay for the universe creation, garment digitisation, and campaign placement. As Doji's user base grows, this fee scales up. Five exclusive brand universes per quarter at $50k average = $1M/quarter in partnership revenue.
◉
Premium Subscription
$12–18/mo
Unlocks advanced features: full Style Chapter archive, unlimited style eras, priority avatar regeneration, early brand drops. At 5% conversion of MAU to premium — standard for social/fashion apps — 500 paying users at $15/mo = $90k MRR, $1.08M ARR at modest scale.
◌
Trend Intelligence Data
$50k–$500k
Anonymised, aggregated style behaviour data is extraordinarily valuable to brands. What colours are 22-30 year olds trying on but not buying? Which silhouettes are saved vs. purchased? This is first-party behavioural data that no survey or focus group can capture. Sold as quarterly brand insights reports.

04 — 90-day execution

What I'd actually
do as an intern

Not a strategy deck. A working rhythm. The kind of intern who makes the team better every week, not just at the end of a project.

1
Weeks 1–2 · Listen before speaking
Absorb everything, suggest nothing

Read every Slack I have access to. QA the app every day, building a living bug doc with UX implications attached. Understand the arguments that shaped existing decisions before I question them. Come in with hypotheses, hold them lightly.

2
Weeks 3–5 · QA as product research
Every bug is a user expectation being violated

I document bugs not just as reproduction steps but as broken user mental models. On SwapCircle, a bug in our swap flow became a full UX redesign because I asked "what did the user expect would happen?" rather than just "what went wrong?" I'll bring that lens here — and flag when a fix reveals a deeper design question worth discussing.

3
Weeks 4–8 · User research cadence
2 interviews/week, synthesised into 1 Slack post

I'll propose a standing rhythm: two user interviews weekly, rotating across new users, power users, and churned users post-avatar-creation. I've run this format before (SwapCircle MVP study, Buenos Aires) — the key is the synthesis. No 30-slide deck. One paragraph in Slack every Friday with the single most important thing I heard that week.

4
Weeks 6–12 · Own one project end-to-end
From insight to shipped. Even small.

I want to take one user insight through the full loop — from interview quote to shipped thing to measured result. My candidate: the biometric safety brief. I'll run the research, write the brief, and advocate for it in whatever forum is right. Whether it gets built while I'm there is secondary. The practice of closing the loop is what matters.

5
Ongoing · Put the product in front of people
Make reels. Read the comments. Iterate.

The fastest and most honest user research channel Doji has access to right now is social media — and I'd push to use it seriously. Short-form videos showing exactly how the try-on works, what's possible with different garment types, behind-the-scenes of avatar creation. Not polished brand content — real demos that invite real reactions.

The comments section is a free, unfiltered usability study. "Why does my skirt look longer?" "Can I try a hijab?" "It made me look smaller than I am." Every complaint is a bug report. Every question is a missing feature. Every "omg this is so cool" is confirmation of what's working. I'd screenshot and tag everything into a running insights doc, and bring the patterns to the team weekly. The product improves faster when you stop only talking to users you recruited and start listening to the ones who showed up on their own.

05 — Why me, specifically

What makes me
unusual for this role

I build for people who weren't in the original user research

I'm Pakistani. I founded Khwaish Shop — a platform connecting underprivileged Pakistani artisans to global buyers. I've spent years designing for contexts where the "default user" assumption fails. At Doji, that translates into a standing habit of asking: who does the avatar not work for yet, and what would it cost to fix it?

I close the loop between user research and shipped code

At SwapCircle I'm both product designer and frontend lead. I've run user interviews that reshuffled our feature roadmap, and then built the features that came out of them. That loop — listen, synthesize, make, measure — is the one I've practiced most. At a small team, it's not a nice-to-have.

I think in systems and stories simultaneously

The revenue flywheel above is systems thinking. The Style Chapter manifesto concept is storytelling. Good product work requires both simultaneously — knowing the business case for a feature without losing sight of how it feels to the person using it. This case study is an attempt to demonstrate both in the same document.
